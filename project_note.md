# Assignment 3 Project Note

In this assignment we wanted to understand how defense-sector firms changed their risk language after 2022. We were not trying to measure whether risks objectively increased in the world, but to map how public companies described risk inside a very specific disclosure genre. We therefore built the corpus from SEC EDGAR rather than from company websites, news coverage, or investor presentations. The corpus is limited to annual 10-K filings and only to Item 1A Risk Factors. This gave us a relatively stable textual setting: the same kind of document, written under the same legal disclosure regime, across a defined set of defense-related firms.

The pipeline has three main parts. The scraper resolves tickers through SEC data, downloads 10-K filings, extracts Item 1A, and writes section-level and paragraph-level files. The cleaner then turns the extracted paragraphs into analysis-ready units. It removes some filing noise and introductory boilerplate, drops risk-summary blocks when they duplicate fuller discussion, merges short heading-like risk statements with their explanatory paragraphs, repairs broken paragraph continuations, and splits bullet lists since we assume each bullet functions as a separate risk claim. This gives us a corpus that is much more granular than full filings, but still traceable back to the source filing, source URL, source paragraph IDs, company, year, and company layer.

The main design choice is the unit of analysis. The unit of analysis is not the whole filing, but a cleaned paragraph-level row: usually one Item 1A paragraph, sometimes a heading merged with its explanatory paragraph, and sometimes an individual bullet item split out from a list.
A filing-level embedding would mix all topics together in one big mush, which would produce a result. but an uninterpretable one. Paragraph-level analysis gives better thematic split. But it also creates the main methodological vulnerability of the project: the distribution of rows is not neutral. A company with long, detailed, or repetitive risk disclosures contributes more points to the embedding space than a company with shorter disclosure. As a heuristic, we assume this reflects the actual risks that should be mentioned in the 1A section of the 10K form. More complex companies have more risks to disclose. This could very easily be wrong. 

In the analysis stage we embed the rows with `BAAI/bge-m3` through SentenceTransformers. The current Colab workflow can run the full corpus by setting `--sample-per-period 0`, while the same script can also run balanced per-period samples. The core report embeds the selected rows once, clusters the pre-2022 and post-2022 subsets separately with UMAP and HDBSCAN, and then approximately matches post clusters back to pre clusters. The matching is not a supervised classification step. It compares cluster centroids with cosine similarity and adds a small top-term overlap signal from TF-IDF. This is useful because the post-2022 period is allowed to organize itself, rather than being forced into the same cluster structure as 2018-2021.

Depending on the run, the precise number of clusters and the match labels can shift. Which is interesting. Probably has to do with the way we construct clusters from the embedding space. It is mostly stable, but some runs show a difference. 

That result changed how we read the project. At first it was tempting to look for new themes after 2022: Ukraine, supply-chain shocks, inflation, cyber escalation, AI, climate disclosure, and industrial-base pressure. Some of these do appear more explicitly in the post period. But the report suggests that they often enter through old disclosure families. Cyber language absorbs ransomware, third-party vendors, and AI-related vulnerabilities. Fixed-price contract language absorbs inflation, labor shortages, supplier pricing, and supply-chain disruption. Environmental language separates more clearly into traditional hazardous waste and newer climate, greenhouse gas, ESG, and disclosure-mandate language. Legal risk becomes more specific around government investigations, False Claims Act exposure, debarment, suspension, and export privileges.

This is where the intra-cluster churn problem becomes central. A cluster can look stable while its internal contents change substantially. If we only ask whether a post cluster has a high-similarity pre analogue, we risk concluding that nothing changed.
The Gemini-assisted layer is our attempt to make the embedding result readable. It does not send the whole corpus to Gemini. It reads the saved clustering artifacts and builds an evidence package for selected post clusters. Each package includes central examples near the centroid, mid-distance examples, peripheral examples, and matched pre-2022 examples. The prompt asks for a restrained interpretation: whether the cluster is mostly continuous, internally shifted, structurally changed, genuinely new, or too weak to interpret. It also asks for layer readings, concentration warnings, temporal readings, category blending, and an explicit note on intra-cluster churn.

This evidence design is useful, but it is has some issues. The central examples are central in embedding space, not necessarily representative in a human interpretive sense. The mid and peripheral examples test whether the theme survives away from the centroid, but they are still selected automatically by distance percentile. The matched pre examples are limited, usually only a few examples from the nearest pre cluster. This means the report can show plausible intra-cluster churn, but it cannot prove a complete cluster-wide transformation.
