# Assignment 3 Project Note

In this assignment we wanted to understand how defense-sector firms changed their risk language after 2022. We were not trying to measure whether risks objectively increased in the world, but to map how public companies described risk inside a very specific disclosure genre. We therefore built the corpus from SEC EDGAR rather than from company websites, news coverage, or investor presentations. The corpus is limited to annual 10-K filings and only to Item 1A Risk Factors. This gave us a relatively stable textual setting: the same kind of document, written under the same legal disclosure regime, across a defined set of defense-related firms.

The corpus was built around U.S.-listed firms with visible defense or national-security exposure. We separated the company universe into primes and suppliers because we expected the two layers to narrate risk differently. Large primes are more exposed to procurement, fixed-price contracts, government audits, program execution, and subcontractor management. Suppliers are more likely to show stress around materials, manufacturing capacity, specialty components, and dependence on larger customers. The final dataset contains 20,625 cleaned analysis units from 245 filings across 33 companies, covering 2018-2025. The comparison window is 2018-2021 versus 2022-2025.

The pipeline has three main parts. The scraper resolves tickers through SEC data, downloads 10-K filings, extracts Item 1A, and writes section-level and paragraph-level files. The cleaner then turns the extracted paragraphs into analysis-ready units. It removes some filing noise and introductory boilerplate, drops risk-summary blocks when they duplicate fuller discussion, merges short heading-like risk statements with their explanatory paragraphs, repairs broken paragraph continuations, and splits bullet lists when each bullet functions as a separate risk claim. This gave us a corpus that is more granular than full filings, but still traceable back to the source filing, source URL, source paragraph IDs, company, year, and company layer.

The main design choice is the unit of analysis. We do not embed whole filings. We embed cleaned risk claims. This is useful because Item 1A sections are long and often contain many different risk themes inside the same filing. A filing-level embedding would blur fixed-price contracts, cyber incidents, environmental regulation, export controls, labor shortages, and shareholder risk into one vector. Paragraph-level analysis gives better thematic resolution. But it also creates the main methodological vulnerability of the project: the distribution of rows is not neutral. A company with long, detailed, or repetitive risk disclosures contributes more points to the embedding space than a company with shorter risk language. Prime contractors also dominate the final dataset, with 16,255 prime rows versus 4,370 supplier rows. That imbalance is analytically meaningful, but it also means supplier-heavy risks can be easier to miss unless we deliberately check layer composition and top-ticker concentration.

In the analysis stage we embed the rows with `BAAI/bge-m3` through SentenceTransformers. The current Colab workflow can run the full corpus by setting `--sample-per-period 0`, while the same script can also run balanced per-period samples. The core report embeds the selected rows once, clusters the pre-2022 and post-2022 subsets separately with UMAP and HDBSCAN, and then approximately matches post clusters back to pre clusters. The matching is not a supervised classification step. It compares cluster centroids with cosine similarity and adds a small top-term overlap signal from TF-IDF. This is useful because the post-2022 period is allowed to organize itself, rather than being forced into the same cluster structure as 2018-2021.

This design makes the project exploratory rather than definitive. Depending on the run, the precise number of clusters and the match labels can shift. That is partly because UMAP and HDBSCAN are sensitive to sampling, random state, density, and parameters such as `cluster_min_size`. But it is also because risk-factor language itself is continuous and boilerplate-heavy. Many disclosures are not cleanly separated into natural topic boxes. They reuse old language, add a few new named risks, and slightly reshape existing paragraphs over time. In that kind of corpus, unstable cluster boundaries are not just a technical problem. They are also a clue that the underlying genre changes gradually and through revision rather than by producing completely new categories.

The strongest current full-corpus report is interesting because it does not show a simple increase in new post-2022 topics. In that run, the model finds 39 non-noise clusters before 2022 and 39 non-noise clusters after 2022. The net cluster gap is zero. Across the post clusters, the report classifies 15 as split/refined, 5 as merged, 19 as persistent, and 0 as clean `new_post_only` themes. At the broader-theme level, however, the number of clusters clearing the broad-theme screen moves from 19 pre clusters to 27 post clusters. This suggests that the post-2022 change is not mainly the appearance of entirely detached new risk categories. It looks more like the same disclosure universe becoming more internally differentiated.

That result changed how we read the project. At first it was tempting to look for new themes after 2022: Ukraine, supply-chain shocks, inflation, cyber escalation, AI, climate disclosure, and industrial-base pressure. Some of these do appear more explicitly in the post period. But the report suggests that they often enter through old disclosure families. Cyber language absorbs ransomware, third-party vendors, and AI-related vulnerabilities. Fixed-price contract language absorbs inflation, labor shortages, supplier pricing, and supply-chain disruption. Environmental language separates more clearly into traditional hazardous waste and newer climate, greenhouse gas, ESG, and disclosure-mandate language. Legal risk becomes more specific around government investigations, False Claims Act exposure, debarment, suspension, and export privileges. The change is real, but it often happens inside stable corporate risk templates.

This is where the intra-cluster churn problem becomes central. A cluster can look stable geometrically while its internal contents change substantially. If we only ask whether a post cluster has a high-similarity pre analogue, we risk concluding that nothing changed. But a stable cyber cluster can still move from generic cybersecurity language toward ransomware, AI, vendors, and disclosure rules. A stable contract cluster can still move from generic cost-overrun language toward inflation, labor, and supplier shocks. This is why the second-stage report asks Gemini to read not only the cluster match, but also examples from different parts of the cluster.

The Gemini-assisted layer is our attempt to make the embedding result readable. It does not send the whole corpus to Gemini. It reads the saved clustering artifacts and builds an evidence package for selected post clusters. Each package includes central examples near the centroid, mid-distance examples, peripheral examples, and matched pre-2022 examples. The prompt asks for a restrained interpretation: whether the cluster is mostly continuous, internally shifted, structurally changed, genuinely new, or too weak to interpret. It also asks for layer readings, concentration warnings, temporal readings, category blending, and an explicit note on intra-cluster churn.

This evidence design is useful, but it is not neutral. The central examples are central in embedding space, not necessarily representative in a human interpretive sense. The mid and peripheral examples test whether the theme survives away from the centroid, but they are still selected automatically by distance percentile. The matched pre examples are limited, usually only a few examples from the nearest pre cluster. This means the report can show plausible intra-cluster churn, but it cannot prove a complete cluster-wide transformation. A stronger version would manually review stratified samples from the most important clusters and compare differential terms across all rows in matched cluster families.

The prime/supplier layer is one of the more useful parts of the project. The current report finds several strongly prime-concentrated themes, including prime and subcontractor teaming dynamics, fixed-price contract and cost-overrun exposure, DCAA/DCMA audit scrutiny, and Foreign Military Sales complexity. This supports the decision to keep company-layer metadata in the corpus. The sector does not speak with one voice. Some post-2022 risk language belongs especially to top-tier firms managing large government programs and lower-tier subcontractors. Other risks may be more visible among upstream firms, but those are easier to understate because suppliers contribute fewer rows overall.

If we continued this project, the first thing to build would be a stability appendix. We would rerun the clustering across several seeds, sample sizes, and `cluster_min_size` values, then distinguish findings that survive from findings that depend on a specific run. We would also add a row-weighting experiment so that firms with unusually long filings do not dominate the semantic space. Another useful addition would be near-duplicate tracking across years and firms. SEC risk factors often evolve by lightly editing old paragraphs, so measuring boilerplate reuse directly would help separate genuine thematic change from denser or more explicit wording.

We would also like to compare matched cluster families more systematically. The current report relies heavily on selected evidence examples, which is good for interpretation but weak as proof. A more robust version would compute pre/post term shifts inside each matched cluster family, check whether named risks such as semiconductors, inflation, ransomware, AI, climate, and debarment increase across the whole cluster, and then use the selected examples only as readable illustrations. That would make the intra-cluster churn argument stronger because it would combine close reading with a cluster-wide lexical check.

Overall, the pipeline is useful as exploratory infrastructure. It is good at surfacing semantic neighborhoods, showing how post-2022 disclosure language reorganizes, and producing traceable evidence packages for close reading. It is weaker as a method for proving stable cluster identities or causal changes in the defense sector. The safest interpretation is that we are studying disclosure behavior: how firms update legally cautious risk language after 2022. The most interesting result is that many new concerns do not appear as cleanly new categories. They enter through older corporate risk templates, making the post-2022 corpus look less like a new map and more like a denser revision of the old one.
